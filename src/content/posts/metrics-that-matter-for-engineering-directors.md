### What to measure, what to ignore, and how to avoid vanity KPIs

Engineering leaders love metrics. Or at least we love the idea of them. The trouble is that once an organization grows past a certain size, every dashboard starts to look like a Christmas tree. Everything is blinking, everything seems important, and half the numbers contradict the other half. At that point, the real job is figuring out which metrics actually help you run the organization and which ones are just there to make someone feel productive.

This is a practical guide to choosing the former and avoiding the latter.

---

## The purpose of metrics at the director level

Metrics should do three things.

- Reveal whether teams are delivering value at a sustainable pace  
- Highlight where the system is slowing down or breaking  
- Inform decisions that actually change outcomes  

If a metric does not help you do one of these, it is probably a distraction. Or worse, a vanity KPI that looks impressive but tells you nothing useful. Think of it like counting the number of meetings held. It is a number. It is measurable. It is also completely useless unless your goal is to win an award for Most Time Spent Talking.

---

## Metrics that actually matter

### Delivery flow
Flow metrics show how work moves through the system. They help you understand whether teams are shipping predictably or whether everything is stuck in a swamp of reviews, dependencies, and well intentioned process.

- Lead time  
- Cycle time  
- Throughput  
- Work in progress  

These metrics tell you how fast teams can turn ideas into working software. They also reveal bottlenecks without requiring you to sit in every standup like a ghost of process past.

### Quality and stability
Quality metrics are not about perfection. They are about whether your teams can ship without waking up the on call engineer at two in the morning.

- Incident frequency  
- Mean time to recovery  
- Change failure rate  
- Escaped defects  

If these numbers trend in the wrong direction, you do not need a meeting to know something is off. You need to invest in testing, observability, or architecture before the system starts sending up smoke signals.

### Team health
Healthy teams ship better software. Unhealthy teams ship surprises. The trick is to measure health without turning it into a corporate trust fall exercise.

- Team satisfaction  
- Attrition trends  
- Onboarding time  
- Manager span of control  

These metrics help you spot burnout, overloaded managers, and teams that are quietly drowning under the weight of legacy systems.

### Strategic alignment
Directors are responsible for making sure teams are not just busy but busy doing the right things.

- Percentage of work tied to strategic initiatives  
- Investment mix across new features, tech debt, and platform work  
- Roadmap predictability  

These metrics help you avoid the classic trap where every team is shipping something but no one can explain how it helps the business.

---

## Metrics you should ignore

### Lines of code
More code is not better. It is usually just more code to maintain. If someone proudly reports that the team wrote fifty thousand lines last quarter, the correct response is mild concern.

### Story points completed
Story points are a planning tool, not a performance metric. Using them to measure productivity is like using the number of grocery bags to measure how good dinner will taste.

### Number of pull requests
A high number of pull requests does not mean a team is productive. It might just mean they are slicing work into tiny pieces or arguing about formatting.

### Meeting counts
If you are measuring meetings, something has gone terribly wrong.

---

## How to avoid vanity KPIs

Vanity KPIs are metrics that look impressive but do not drive decisions. They usually share a few traits.

- They trend upward forever  
- They are easy to manipulate  
- They make someone feel good without improving anything  
- They do not change what you do next  

The best way to avoid them is to ask one simple question: If this number changes, will I make a different decision? If the answer is no, delete it from the dashboard and enjoy the extra screen space.

---

## Putting it all together

A good director level metrics stack is small, boring, and relentlessly tied to outcomes. It should help you see whether teams are delivering value, whether the system is healthy, and whether the organization is moving in the right direction. Everything else is noise.

Before you build your next dashboard, it is worth asking which of your current metrics you trust and which ones you secretly ignore. That answer usually tells you where to start cleaning things up.
