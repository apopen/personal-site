"We need a design system." Every startup says this. Most fail to deliver.

Here's why they matter, and how to actually build one that works.

## What Design Systems Actually Provide

**Consistency** - Users learn your product once. Buttons work the same everywhere. Forms behave predictably. This is user experience.

**Speed** - Developers don't rebuild the same components. Designers don't redraw the same elements. Everything gets faster.

**Communication** - "Use the design system" is a complete sentence. No endless debates about button colors.

## The Trap

Design systems become their own project. Teams build systems for systems. The main product suffers while people polish buttons nobody uses.

Don't do this.

## What Actually Works

**Start with what you have** - Extract common patterns from your existing UI. Don't design hypothetical components.

**Grow organically** - Add components as you need them. Not everything needs to be in version 1.0.

**Make it accessible** - A design system that excludes users isn't a system. It's decoration.

**Maintain it** - Someone owns it. Bugs get fixed. Updates get shipped. Otherwise it rots.

## The Real Benefit

A good design system isn't a component library. It's a shared language. It lets designers and developers work at speed without stepping on each other.

That's the win.
