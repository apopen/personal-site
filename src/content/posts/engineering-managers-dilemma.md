You're in a meeting. Two senior engineers are debating the merits of a microservices migration versus modular monolith. They look at you expectantly. You need to make a call.

But you're also supposed to be managing people. Writing performance reviews. Making sure the team is happy. How do you balance being technical with being a manager?

## The Fake It Till You Make It Trap

Some EM's fake technical depth. They nod along in architecture meetings, agree with whoever spoke last, and hope nobody notices.

This works for a while. Then someone asks a hard question. The team loses trust. Game over.

## The Better Path

**Stay technical enough to ask good questions** - You don't need to write the code. You need to understand enough to know if the plan makes sense.

**Build a technical advisory board** - Your senior engineers are your technical brain. Use them. Trust them.

**Admit what you don't know** - "I'm not sure, let's figure that out together" builds more trust than pretending.

**Focus on outcomes, not code** - Your job is making sure the right work gets done, not doing the work yourself.

## The Real Answer

The best engineering managers I've seen are technical enough to be respected, but managerial enough to let go. They know their limits. They build strong teams. They focus on removing obstacles.

That's the job. It's harder than it sounds.
