Every engineering team has been there. Someone from leadership asks for metrics, and suddenly you're tracking everything that moves. Lines of code. PR count. Ticket closure rate.

Here's the thing though - most of these metrics are about as useful as a screen door on a submarine.

## The Dashboard Decorations

**Lines of Code** - This is the classic one. More lines does not mean more value. It probably means more complexity and more bugs. If anything, fewer lines is usually the win.

**PR Count** - Oh look, Sarah opened 47 PRs this week while Bob opened 12. Sarah must be crushing it. Unless Bob's 12 PRs are substantial features and Sarah's 47 are one-line typo fixes.

**Ticket Velocity** - Moving tickets from "In Progress" to "Done" quickly is great, unless we're just playing a game of column-hopping without actually solving problems.

## The Ones That Actually Matter

**Cycle Time** - How long from when someone starts working on something to when it's in production. This captures the real heartbeat of your team.

**Escaped Bugs** - What actually made it to users? This is uncomfortably honest, which is exactly why it's valuable.

**On-Call Incidents** - Are people getting paged at 3am too often? That burnout metric tends to get leadership's attention real quick.

**DORA Metrics** - Deployment frequency, lead time, change failure rate, and mean time to recovery. These four actually correlate with high-performing teams. Shocking, I know.

## The Bottom Line

If your metric can be gamed without improving anything real, it's not a metric. It's a participation trophy.

The best teams focus on outcomes, not output. Your dashboard might look impressive, but if users are still complaining and your team is burning out, those numbers are just pretty decoration.
